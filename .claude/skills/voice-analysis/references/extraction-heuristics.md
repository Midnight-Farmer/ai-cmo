# Extraction Heuristics

Concrete rules for what counts as a signature phrase, a rhythm pattern, a tone shift, and so on. The goal is reproducibility: two analysts working from the same corpus should produce similar outputs.

---

## Pre-processing the corpus

Before counting anything, clean the text. Otherwise frequencies get polluted by noise.

**Always strip:**
- YAML frontmatter (between leading `---` markers)
- Code blocks (between triple backticks) — these are syntax, not voice
- Whisper transcription artifacts: `[inaudible]`, `[crosstalk]`, lone single letters at line starts, sequences like "the the the"
- Quoted material from other authors. In blog posts, this usually appears as italicized blockquotes (`> *...*` or `> ...`). In transcripts, this is usually obvious by context ("as Charlie Munger said,...").
- Scripture quotations (often in italic blockquote form)
- URL strings, file paths, email addresses

**Preserve:**
- Wiki-links (`[[Note Name]]`) as-is — but the words inside them count as the client's own words only if the client wrote that note's title themselves; treat conservatively (don't count link text in phrase frequency)
- Em-dashes, ellipses, parentheticals — these are punctuation evidence, especially their absence
- Contractions (don't, it's, that's) — these are voice texture
- Sentence-starting conjunctions — these are voice texture

**Do NOT lowercase the text** before searching for phrases like "I" vs "i" or "AI" vs "ai" — case is sometimes meaningful. For phrase frequency counting, do case-insensitive matching but preserve the original case in the cited example.

## What counts as a "signature phrase"

A phrase is signature when it meets all three:

1. **Exact or near-exact match** — same words in the same order, allowing for small inflectional changes (singular/plural, verb tense). "brick by brick" and "brick by brick by brick" share a stem and count as the same phrase. "the bucket" and "buckets" do not — those are common nouns; you need a longer carrier phrase.
2. **Frequency ≥ 3 across the corpus**, OR ≥ 2 if it occurs in distinct contexts (one blog, one transcript) AND it's distinctive (not a common English bigram).
3. **At least 2 distinct source files.** A phrase that appears 5 times in one transcript and nowhere else is a tic of one moment, not a signature.

**Length:** 2-7 words is the sweet spot. Single words go in the Vocabulary section; longer phrases are usually paraphrases dressed as quotes — break them into the smaller signature inside.

**Exception — personal openers/closers:** Recurring openers ("Look,", "Here's the thing,", "So,") and closers ("That's it.", "That's the whole thing.", "Brick by brick.") are signature even at frequency 2 if they appear in distinct sources. Note them with their function ("opener" or "closer") in the Notes column.

**Reject as signature:**
- Common English idioms (e.g. "at the end of the day") unless their use is distinctively heavy in this corpus AND they don't appear in the "Avoided" list of the brand
- Quotes from other people that the client repeated
- Brand/product names — those go in a separate "Vocabulary > Used" row
- Phrases that only appear in YAML, captions auto-generated by tools, or boilerplate

**Counting near-matches:**

| Variant 1 | Variant 2 | Same phrase? |
|-----------|-----------|--------------|
| "brick by brick" | "brick by brick by brick" | Yes — stem repeats |
| "open to close" | "from open to close" | Yes — same metaphor, count both forms; cite both |
| "steward well" | "stewardship" | No — different surface forms; the second is single-word, goes in Vocabulary |
| "I don't know" | "I really don't know" | Yes — same stem, treat the modifier as variant |

When in doubt, list both forms in the Phrase column separated by `/` and combine the count: `"brick by brick" / "brick by brick by brick" — count 7`.

## What counts as a "rhythm / sentence pattern"

A pattern is real when:

1. It recurs across at least 3 sentences, AND
2. The recurring sentences come from at least 2 source files, AND
3. The pattern can be described in a single short sentence (if it takes a paragraph to define, it's probably not a pattern — it's a category)

**Patterns to actively check for:**

| Pattern | Definition | Signal |
|---------|-----------|--------|
| Short-then-long | A 2-7 word sentence followed by a 20+ word sentence | Look for very short standalone sentences immediately followed by long ones |
| Sentence-starting conjunctions | Sentences that begin with And, But, So, Because, Or, Yet | Count the rate; ≥5% of sentences = a pattern |
| Parenthetical asides | Mid-sentence interruptions in `( )`, ` — `, or commas-as-interruption | Count occurrences per 1000 words |
| One-sentence paragraphs | A paragraph with exactly one sentence | Count the rate; ≥10% = a pattern |
| Rule of three | Three parallel items in a sentence ("X, Y, and Z") with parallel grammar | At least 4 instances across the corpus |
| Repetition for rhythm | Word/phrase repeated 3+ times in close succession ("brick by brick by brick") | At least 3 instances across the corpus |
| Question-answer | A rhetorical question immediately followed by its answer ("Why? Because...") | At least 4 instances across the corpus |
| List-as-paragraph | Sentence containing a bulleted-feeling enumeration without bullets | At least 3 instances |
| Self-correction (spoken only) | "Actually—", "no wait,", "what I mean is" | At least 4 instances in spoken sources |

**Do not invent patterns.** If you only see something twice, it's an instance, not a pattern. Add it to a "watch list" in Notes on Confidence so the next run can confirm.

**Always check the em-dash question.** Does the client use em-dashes? Most AI prose does. If em-dashes are absent (count: 0), that is one of the most useful patterns to record — see "What They Don't Say".

## Vocabulary: Used

A word/phrase qualifies for the "Used" list when:

1. It appears 4+ times across the corpus (lower bar than signature phrases because we accept single words here), OR
2. It carries thematic weight even at lower frequency (theme markers — words that name the brand's pillars)
3. It is not a common English word that appears in everyone's writing ("the", "and", "people"). Skip the top 1000 most common English words.

For each entry, supply:
- Exact word/phrase
- Occurrence count
- One example sentence with citation
- A theme tag (e.g. "faith", "family", "stewardship", "building", "grit") when applicable

Tag thematically when the word ties to a brand pillar. The user's `voice-guidelines.md` will define the pillars — read it for reference but do not copy from it.

## Vocabulary: Avoided

A word/phrase qualifies for the "Avoided" list when:

1. It is a common AI-tell, marketing buzzword, or genre cliché in the relevant genre (business, fitness, marketing, etc.), AND
2. It has 0 occurrences in the corpus, AND
3. The corpus is large enough that the absence is meaningful (≥5,000 words)

**Default check list — every voice analysis should test these:**

- "leverage" (verb usage)
- "unlock" (verb usage)
- "delve"
- "elevate"
- "robust"
- "seamless"
- "navigate" (metaphorical)
- "in today's [adjective] world"
- "it's no secret that"
- "in this post we'll explore"
- "the bottom line is"
- em-dash ( — ) — count occurrences (not just absence)
- ellipsis ( … ) — count occurrences
- exclamation point — count rate per 1000 words

**Genre-specific additions:**

- For finance/CFO clients: add "synergy", "right-size", "deep dive", "best-in-class"
- For fitness/coaching: add "transform your life", "level up", "unlock your potential"
- For SaaS/tech: add "ecosystem", "supercharge", "next-gen"
- For faith-aligned content: be sensitive — terms like "blessed", "anointed" may be authentic for some clients and clichéd for others. Do not auto-add to either list; let the corpus decide.

For each entry, record:
- The word/phrase
- Corpus count (0 for absent)
- A short note on what the client says instead, with a citation to the substitute

Example:
> "leverage" — 0 occurrences in 47,212 words. Says "use" instead: "use the planning meeting to set the year" (`+/Planning as a Tool.md:24`).

## Tone variations

A "context" qualifies for its own tone-variations entry when:

1. There are at least 2 source files of that type in the corpus, OR
2. The single source is large (>3,000 words)

**Contexts to consider:** long-form (blog/essay), short-form (social captions, X posts, IG copy), spoken (video transcripts, podcast transcripts, voice notes), conversational (meeting transcripts, casual recordings).

**Metrics to compute per context:**

- **Average sentence length** — total words / total sentences (split on `.!?` followed by whitespace; ignore sentences inside parentheses)
- **First-person rate** — % of sentences starting with "I", "I'm", "I've", "My", "We're", etc.
- **Opening style ratio** — of the first sentences in each piece, what % are stories (concrete moment) vs claims (assertion)?
- **Question rate** — questions per 1000 words

If you cannot compute a metric in-context (corpus too large, computation too tedious), mark it "approximate" and explain. Do not invent numbers.

## What they don't say

This is the proofreader's list. The bar is high: every entry must be backed by a count of 0 in a sufficiently large corpus, AND the entry must be a thing copywriters/AI commonly use. The list is most useful when it has 8-15 entries — long enough to catch real drift, short enough to scan during a proofread.

Always cite the substitute (what the client says instead) so a reviewer can see the contrast.

---

## Confidence rules

Be honest about corpus quality. Set the `status:` to `needs-update` if any of these are true:

- Corpus < 5,000 words → frequency counts are unreliable; analysis is preliminary
- Single-source dominance — one file contributes >50% of total words
- All material is from one context (e.g. all transcripts, no blog) — tone variations cannot be derived
- All material is from a narrow date range (single week) — may miss seasonal voice shifts

Otherwise, `status: active`.

In "Notes on Confidence", explicitly list:
- Corpus size and balance
- Gaps
- Run history (date + mode + what was added)
- Overall confidence rating: low / medium / high

## Anti-patterns to avoid

These are mistakes a hurried analyst makes. Don't.

1. **Counting from filenames.** Filenames are descriptors, not the person's words. Always count from the file body.
2. **Treating quoted material as the client's voice.** A blog post quoting Munger is using Munger's words, not the client's. Strip quotes before counting.
3. **Padding the signature phrases list.** If only 5 phrases meet the bar, list 5. Do not invent borderline entries to hit a number.
4. **Generalizing from one source.** A pattern in one transcript is not a pattern in the voice. Need ≥2 sources.
5. **Paraphrasing in citations.** The Notes column may paraphrase; the example column must use exact quotes.
6. **Lower-casing during display.** Even if you match case-insensitively, display the phrase in its dominant or original case in the output.
7. **Listing avoided words you didn't actually check.** False negatives mislead future workflows. Only include "0 occurrences" rows for words you actually grepped.
